How the irrigation estimate is calculated

The daily water need is calculated from forecast temperature, sunlight, humidity, and wind. The estimate accounts for your crop's growth stage — young crops and crops nearing harvest need less water than crops at peak growth. Your soil type adjusts the final number: sandy soils need more frequent watering, clay soils hold moisture longer and need less.

Rain is subtracted from the daily need. If forecast rain covers the day's water demand, no irrigation is suggested for that day. There is no carry-over credit for extra rain beyond that day's need.

The grey band in the chart is built from 5 years of historical weather data for your area, run through the same calculation, so you can see how this season compares to past years at the same time of year.

Use this as a planning guide alongside your own observations of field conditions.

Reading this chart

  • Grey band — Shows how much irrigation similar seasons in past years needed by each day. A narrow band means past years were consistent — you can plan with more confidence. A wide band means some years were much drier or wetter than others — treat the outlook as a rough guide, not a firm number.
  • Dashed line — What a typical past season looked like. If the teal line is tracking above it, this season is shaping up drier than normal. Below it, wetter than normal.
  • Teal line — Your current forecast. Running near the top of the grey band means prepare for more irrigation than usual. Near the bottom, you may need less than a typical year.
  • Beyond day 7 — The forecast becomes less reliable. The wider the band, the more you should hold off on firm decisions until you are closer to the date.
